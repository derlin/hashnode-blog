Want to know more about me? Here is a condensed summary of my story as a Software Developer.

## How I fell in love with programming

I discovered programming quite late. I was more of a bookworm in my teens and loathed technology. We had a computer at home, but I never really used it. I just remember watching my sister chat on caramail, and dictating my handwritten reports to my mum so she could turn them into Word documents.

After graduating college, I started university in Human Sciences (Sociology, Communication, and a minor in Philosophy), which required only basic knowledge in Google and Word, sometimes SPSS (for statistics). My roommate also showed me how to watch animes (using MegaUpload), but that was pretty much it.

As Human Sciences didn't open a lot of opportunities, I planned a Master's in Information Technologies. I didn't really understand what it meant, just that it would lead to job opportunities. I had to take some extra credits first, and since I had time to spare, I went as a free auditor to random classes. I stumbled upon one called "*Programming in Java*": not understanding half of the title, I gathered it would be fun. This was a revelation.

The class was based solely on [Java notes](https://math.hws.edu/javanotes/) (version 5 at the time) and after the first week, I printed the whole book/site and started going through it every night, one exercise after the other. It didn't take me long: around 2 months.

I slowly realized that computers weren't my enemies, here on earth only to make me miserable. Instead, I discovered a world of logic, order, determinism (well, this is not true, but when you start it can feel like it), and most of all beauty. I was suddenly able to create things, to control technology, this was awesome.

I dropped my Master's and went to the opposite side of the road, where stood an Engineering School:

> *Hi, my name is Lucy, I know nothing but I am really motivated*.

They accepted me despite my lack of experience (I was a girl, and finished my first Bachelor's as the valedictorian, which helped), and I thus started anew, at a Bachelor's level. This was in 2011.

## My Master's

## My aborted Doctorate

After my Master's, I had a choice to make: stay at Berkeley, or move back home and start a Doctorate with Swisscom - the biggest telecommunications provider in Switzerland. Swisscom usually didn't pair with Engineering Schools (only universities), but they were impressed by one of my projects and wanted to make an exception. I always wanted to be able to wear a "*not that kind of doctor*" shirt, so I went for the Doctorate.

The Doctorate's goal was to build an automatic translation tool from Swiss German to high German using NLP and neural networks (I don't speak either 😅). There is close to no data on Swiss German, so the first task was to collect a large enough corpus to start building models.

I created [swisstext](https://github.com/derlin/swisstext), a tool written in Python to semi-automatically gather Swiss-German sentences from the web. This gave raise to the biggest corpus to date, SwissCrawl, which is described in the paper [Automatic Creation of Text Corpora for Low-Resource Languages from the Internet: The Case of Swiss German](https://arxiv.org/abs/1912.00159) .

%[https://github.com/derlin/swisstext] 

The Doctorate was going well. The only problem? Doing Machine Learning made me miserable. I realized this didn't match my mentality at all. With ML, you mostly work with a black box. The model doesn't work? It may be the data, the code, the random initialization, or the lack of resources for training. The beauty of the code doesn't matter, as long as the math is correct. And when the model works, you often don't really understand why either. This is definitely not for me: I am the kind of person who needs to understand exactly every piece of code I type, and who enjoys spending time to make the code pretty and efficient.

It took me 6 months to accept this reality. Swisscom, and especially my boss, was amazingly understanding and kind. I left, not without leaving a trace, as my corpus helped Swisscom release a TV box speaking Swiss German.

## And now

I am now a working Software Engineer, and I love it. I am still as awed and excited by programming as I was when I began.

My first job at SICPA taught me about Kubernetes, DevOps, and teamwork (I am now a certified Scrum Master). My plans? Keep learning and enjoying myself, and let my passion drive me to new places.
