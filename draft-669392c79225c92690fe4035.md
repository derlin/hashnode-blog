The Ten Commandments of Egoless Programming are often incorrectly attributed to the book "The Psychology of Computer Programming" by Gerald M. Weinberg (which introduces *the egoless* concept) and less often to "The Pragmatic Programmer" by Andrew Hunt and David Thomas.

"The Pragmatic Programmer," published in 1999, presents these commandments as principles for developing good programming habits and fostering a collaborative and productive team environment (You'll tell me 20+ years is old for a programming book, but if there is one book you have to read as an engineer, this is it!).

In this article, I'd like to briefly present the commandments, and then give my two cents.

## The original commandments

Here are the Ten Commandments of Egoless Programming as presented in "The Pragmatic Programmer" (you many have stumbled upon variations):

1. **Understand and accept that you will make mistakes.** The point is to find them early before they make it into production.
    
2. **You are not your code.** Remember that the entire point of a review is to find problems, and problems will be found. Don't take it personally when one is uncovered.
    
3. **No matter how much "karate" you know, someone else will always know more.** Such an individual can teach you some new moves if you ask.
    
4. **Don't rewrite code without consultation.** There is a fine line between "fixing code" and "rewriting code." The latter can often introduce new bugs.
    
5. **Treat people who know less than you with respect, deference, and patience.** Nontechnical people often have valuable insights.
    
6. **The only constant in the world is change.** Be open to it and adapt to it.
    
7. **The only true authority stems from knowledge, not from position.**
    
8. **Fight for what you believe, but gracefully accept defeat.** Understand why you lost and learn from it.
    
9. **Don't be "the coder in the corner."** Interact with your peers and share your knowledge.
    
10. **Critique code instead of people – be kind to the coder, not to the code.**
    

## A critique of each commandment

### #1 Accept you'll make mistakes

Someone who's never made mistakes has never tried something new. No one, even the best of us, can avoid mistakes. The trick is to put everything in place to find mistakes as early as possible, to limit the blast radius. In today's world, we are not short of tools. To cite a few: PR reviews, encompassing test suites, CI/CDs, QA, blue-green deployments, etc.

Another part of what makes a good programmer is he's attitude towards mistakes. As side rules:

* Always keep in mind that mistakes are a learning opportunity. My most valuable experiences happened while breaking production!
    
* Own your mistakes, but do not blame yourself (this equilibrium is an art). You should always recognize your mistakes - do not hide them from your team or, even worse, put it on another's shoulder (avoid CYAE - *Cover Your Ass Engineering*). But do not fret about them too much either. Again, they are unavoidable and make you grow!
    
* Remember that, for most of us at least, mistakes are rarely fatal (please, skip this if you are developing nuclear plants cooling systems). Learn, fix, laugh, and move on!
    
* A 100% available system doesn't exist - and this is fine. To battle against this misconception, Google goes as far as deliberately taking systems offline occasionally. Likewise, the typical background error rate for ISPs is between 0.01% and 1%. If your product is internet facing, ensuring fewer errors than ISPs is enough to make them unnoticeable.
    
* Accept your humanity. You're not a machine.
    

### #2 You are not your code

*This one is especially hard on me and has a philosophical ring to it. I can't help but associate myself with my actions and outputs!*

Once you accepted you can make mistakes, and are ready to own them, welcoming critiques is the logical next step. Reviews are always tricky, and in my experience, everything comes down to the wording and attitude of the reviewer (see also commandment #10).

Here is some advice for reviewers:

1. Positive feedback is also a thing. Do not only point at mistakes, but highlight what's good in the PR. You see something you would not have thought about? You like the commit message? SAY IT!
    
2. Conveying an opinion or a tone by text is tricky. Not an emoji enthusiast, I use [conventional comments](https://conventionalcomments.org) when doing reviews to avoid undercommunication and misunderstandings. If you know about [conventional commits](https://www.conventionalcommits.org/en/v1.0.0/) (you should), it's the same idea but for reviews. My favorites are `praise:`, `thought:`, `nitpick:`, `question:` and `suggestion:`.
    

### #3 You'll always find better than you

This always reminds me of two psychological phenomena:

1. The [Dunning-Kruger effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dunning%E2%80%93Kruger_effect): the tendency of people with low ability in a specific area to give overly positive assessments of this ability and the reverse, the tendency of highly skilled people to underestimate their abilities relative to the abilities of others.
    
2. The [imposter syndrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impostor_syndrome): "the subjective experience of perceived self-doubt in one's abilities and accomplishments compared with others, despite evidence to suggest the contrary."
    

The best way to counteract those biases comes down - again - to attitude and acceptance. Welcoming knowledge and learn opportunities instead of seeing it as a threat. Asking a question is not a sign of defeat. Learn to say "I don't know" and "can you show me"!

I believe the company culture has much to do with this: 360° and other personal assessments linked to salary raises may be very detrimental. If you are a senior software engineer, the way you treat juniors and answer their questions will also play a big role (commandment #10!).

> When a new programmer enters the milieu, his attitudes may be shaped by the reactions of the others already there. If he goes to somebody for advice and he is ridiculed for the stupidity of his errors, he is less likely to seek assistance the next time. If, however, someone comes to him and asks for help in looking over a program, he is flattered by the implied compliment to his ability and may not feel so threatened when he has to seek advice.
> 
> From "The psychology of computer programming"

### #4 Don't rewrite without consultation

If you believe your changes may improve the code, why limit yourself? I often pondered, and it took me several years of experience to understand this one.

1. Any change conveys the risk of introducing new bugs. Replacing known unknowns with unknown unknowns must be part of a risk assessment taken by the team!
    
2. More often than not, refactoring means making the code better fit your personal preferences or style. If everyone does this, you end up with a constantly changing codebase no one is at ease with.
    
3. It hurts to see your code stripped away without notice. Feelings are also at stake, whatever the second commandment tells you. Think of the original author before any refactoring.
    
4. Some of what you may judge hacky or suboptimal at first glance may be here for a reason. Without discussing it first, you risk spending days before hurting yourself to the same wall people before you already discovered. Instead of refactoring, ensure the wall is explicit and well-documented.
    
5. You are part of a team. As much as we gospel about the "engineering" part of our jobs, there is always a sociological / psychological dimension. Don't play alone.
